The 30-Second Elevator Explanation

One tight paragraph that explains a concept in under 30 seconds.

Works on any free AI  ·  Best tool:  Any AI chat (ChatGPT/Gemini/Claude/Copilot)

✅ How to use this effectively

  1. 1. Open any free AI chat.
  2. 2. Paste the prompt.
  3. 3. Add the concept and audience.
  4. 4. Send and read the paragraph.
  5. 5. Memorise it for quick recall.
🔧 If it is not right, reply with this: If it is too long or technical, reply: "Cut it to 60 words, remove all jargon, and keep only the single most important idea."
THE PROMPT
ROLE: Act as a mathematics teacher who can explain any concept in one tight, memorable paragraph.

CONTEXT: I teach [CLASS/GRADE] for [BOARD or EXAM e.g. CBSE / ICSE / State Board / JEE / NEET]. I want a 30-second elevator explanation of this concept that I can say from memory: [WRITE THE CONCEPT HERE]. Audience: [STUDENTS / PARENTS / A NEW COLLEAGUE].

YOUR TASK:
1. Write ONE paragraph (about 60 to 90 words) that fully captures the concept.
2. Lead with the single most important idea in the first sentence.
3. Use everyday words; introduce at most one essential term.
4. End with a one-line reason the concept matters.
5. Then give a shorter 20-word version for when time is very tight.

INPUT: Here is the concept: [PASTE OR TYPE IT HERE]. Audience: [STUDENTS / PARENTS / COLLEAGUE].

HOW TO WORK IT OUT: Internally check the paragraph against the precise definition to ensure nothing is wrong or misleading. VERIFY by re-reading it twice: once for correctness and once to confirm a non-expert could follow it. Never invent formulas or overclaim. Keep any maths in clear plain words rather than symbols.

OUTPUT FORMAT (use these exact headings):
- CONCEPT
- THE 30-SECOND EXPLANATION (one paragraph)
- WHY IT MATTERS (1 line)
- THE 20-WORD VERSION (one short sentence)

RULES: No equations in the paragraph; words only. Must be true and not oversimplified to the point of being wrong. Should be comfortable to say aloud in one breath group.

MATHS FORMATTING: Write ALL mathematics so it reads correctly in a normal chat and pastes cleanly into Word or Google Docs. Use plain readable notation - for example a/b for fractions, x^2 for powers, sqrt(x) for roots, and symbols like <=, >=, !=, +-, pi, theta, integral and sum. Do NOT output raw LaTeX or code such as \frac{}{}, \sqrt{}, x^{2}, $...$ or \( ... \) - these appear as broken symbols to a teacher in a free chat.

SIGNATURE: At the very end add a footer line: "Prepared by Indrajeet Yadav", and put "Compiled by Indrajeet Yadav" in the title or header block.
