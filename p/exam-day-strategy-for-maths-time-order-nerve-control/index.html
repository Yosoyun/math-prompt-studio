Exam-Day Strategy for Maths (Time, Order & Nerve Control)

A concrete exam-day game plan: which questions to attempt first, time budgets and mistake guards.

Works on any free AI  ·  Best tool:  Any AI chat (ChatGPT/Gemini/Claude/Copilot)

✅ How to use this effectively

  1. Open any AI chat.
  2. Type the exam, time and marking scheme.
  3. Paste the prompt and send.
  4. Give the plan to students before the exam.
  5. Rehearse the time budget in a mock.
  6. For a trustworthy answer key: paste the finished paper into a FRESH chat and ask it to solve and grade it as an unseen exam (it catches its own slips).
🔧 If it is not right, reply with this: If the time budget does not fit the paper, reply: "The minute-by-minute plan exceeds [TIME] - rework it so the total time used is within the limit, keeping a buffer for review."
THE PROMPT
ROLE: Act as an exam-day performance coach for mathematics, expert in time strategy and pressure management.

CONTEXT: My students sit [EXAM e.g. JEE Main / Board Maths] at [CLASS/GRADE] level. The maths paper has about [NUMBER OF QUESTIONS] questions, time [TIME e.g. 3 hours], marking scheme [MARKING e.g. +4/-1 or section-wise]. They lose marks to poor time use and panic, not lack of knowledge.

YOUR TASK:
1. Give a clear ATTEMPT ORDER strategy: which question types to do first, what to skip-and-return, and how to use a first/second/third pass.
2. Build a realistic TIME BUDGET (minutes per section or per question block) that fits inside [TIME] with a review buffer.
3. Provide a MISTAKE-GUARD checklist for the common exam-day slips (misreading, sign errors, OMR/bubbling, not reading 'except/incorrect').
4. Add NERVE-CONTROL tactics: what to do in the first 5 minutes, what to do if stuck, and how to recover after a hard question.
5. Add a final 10-minute end-game routine (review, guess-vs-leave decisions given the marking).

INPUT: Here are the paper details (exam, questions, time, marking): [PASTE DETAILS HERE]. If a field is blank, assume the standard pattern and state the assumption in one line at the top.

HOW TO WORK IT OUT: Make the time budget ADD UP - the total planned minutes plus the review buffer must be less than or equal to [TIME]; show the arithmetic and fix it if it overflows. Tailor the guess-vs-leave advice to the exact marking scheme given (negative marking changes the strategy). Keep advice concrete and actionable, not generic. Keep any maths legible.

OUTPUT FORMAT, in this exact order:

OUTPUT FILE FORMAT: present the complete result as a clean, ready-to-use document I can paste straight into Microsoft Word or Google Docs - a clear bold title, section headings, neatly numbered items, comfortable spacing, and simple tables only where helpful (avoid heavy formatting that breaks on paste). It must also print cleanly to PDF on A4 (File > Print > Save as PDF). Keep all mathematics fully readable (fractions as a/b, powers as x^2, roots as sqrt(x)).

MATHS FORMATTING: Write ALL mathematics so it reads correctly in a normal chat and pastes cleanly into Word or Google Docs. Use plain readable notation - for example a/b for fractions, x^2 for powers, sqrt(x) for roots, and symbols like <=, >=, !=, +-, pi, theta, integral and sum. Do NOT output raw LaTeX or code such as \frac{}{}, \sqrt{}, x^{2}, $...$ or \( ... \) - these appear as broken symbols to a teacher in a free chat.
A. Title block: 'Exam: [EXAM]', 'Maths Exam-Day Strategy', 'Time: [TIME]', 'Compiled by Indrajeet Yadav'.
B. SECTION 1 - ATTEMPT ORDER: the multi-pass plan.
C. SECTION 2 - TIME BUDGET: a table with minutes per block and the arithmetic showing it fits [TIME].
D. SECTION 3 - MISTAKE-GUARD CHECKLIST.
E. SECTION 4 - NERVE CONTROL: first 5 minutes, when stuck, recovery.
F. SECTION 5 - LAST 10 MINUTES: end-game routine and guess-vs-leave rule for this marking scheme.

RULES: Time budget must fit within the limit (show the maths). Guess advice must match the marking scheme. Concrete, not generic. State assumptions.

At the very end add a footer line: 'Prepared by Indrajeet Yadav', and put 'Compiled by Indrajeet Yadav' in the title block.
