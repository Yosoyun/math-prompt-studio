Last-15-Days Revision Plan (Day-by-Day Schedule)

A 15-day day-by-day revision timetable with daily targets, drills and self-tests.

Works on any free AI  ·  Best tool:  Any AI chat (ChatGPT/Gemini/Claude/Copilot)

✅ How to use this effectively

  1. Open any AI chat.
  2. List exam, subjects and weak topics.
  3. Say how many hours per day are free.
  4. Paste the prompt and send.
  5. Print the plan and tick days off.
  6. For a trustworthy answer key: paste the finished paper into a FRESH chat and ask it to solve and grade it as an unseen exam (it catches its own slips).
🔧 If it is not right, reply with this: If a day is overloaded, reply: "Day 6 has too much for [HOURS] hours - rebalance Days 5 to 8 so each fits the available time, keeping total coverage the same."
THE PROMPT
ROLE: Act as a senior exam mentor who designs realistic, high-yield short-term revision plans.

CONTEXT: My student has 15 days left for [EXAM e.g. JEE Main / NEET / Board] at [CLASS/GRADE] level. They can study about [HOURS e.g. 6] focused hours a day. Strong areas: [STRONG TOPICS]. Weak areas: [WEAK TOPICS]. Subjects to cover: [SUBJECTS].

YOUR TASK:
1. Build a day-by-day 15-day revision plan that prioritises high-weightage and weak topics, while keeping strong topics warm.
2. Each day must have: focus topics, a short concept-revision block, a practice/drill block (with a suggested number of questions), and a quick self-test.
3. Schedule at least 3 full mock/section tests across the 15 days, with a review day after each.
4. Keep the daily load within [HOURS] hours and include short breaks and a no-new-topic rule for the final 2 days.

INPUT: Here are the details (exam, class, subjects, strong/weak topics, hours/day): [PASTE DETAILS HERE]. If hours are blank, assume 6 and state it.

HOW TO WORK IT OUT: Make the plan add up - the total study hours per day must not exceed [HOURS]. Double-check that every weak topic and every high-weightage topic appears at least twice across the 15 days (spaced repetition). Do not promise unrealistic coverage. Keep it practical and humane (sleep, breaks). State any assumptions in one line at the top.

OUTPUT FORMAT, in this exact order:

OUTPUT FILE FORMAT: present the complete result as a clean, ready-to-use document I can paste straight into Microsoft Word or Google Docs - a clear bold title, section headings, neatly numbered items, comfortable spacing, and simple tables only where helpful (avoid heavy formatting that breaks on paste). It must also print cleanly to PDF on A4 (File > Print > Save as PDF). Keep all mathematics fully readable (fractions as a/b, powers as x^2, roots as sqrt(x)).

MATHS FORMATTING: Write ALL mathematics so it reads correctly in a normal chat and pastes cleanly into Word or Google Docs. Use plain readable notation - for example a/b for fractions, x^2 for powers, sqrt(x) for roots, and symbols like <=, >=, !=, +-, pi, theta, integral and sum. Do NOT output raw LaTeX or code such as \frac{}{}, \sqrt{}, x^{2}, $...$ or \( ... \) - these appear as broken symbols to a teacher in a free chat.
A. Title block: 'Exam: [EXAM]', 'Plan: Last 15 Days', 'Hours/day: [HOURS]', 'Compiled by Indrajeet Yadav'.
B. SECTION 1 - STRATEGY SUMMARY: 4-6 lines on the approach and the spacing logic.
C. SECTION 2 - DAY-BY-DAY TABLE: Day | Focus topics | Concept block | Practice block (with question count) | Self-test | Hours total.
D. SECTION 3 - MOCK TEST SCHEDULE: which days, what to cover, and review-day notes.
E. SECTION 4 - FINAL 48 HOURS: a calm, no-new-topic checklist.

RULES: Daily hours never exceed the limit. Weak and high-weightage topics revisited (spaced). Realistic and humane. State assumptions.

At the very end add a footer line: 'Prepared by Indrajeet Yadav', and put 'Compiled by Indrajeet Yadav' in the title block.

CONTEXT CHECK: Before you begin, if any [SQUARE-BRACKET] above is still empty, or I have not given you the question / photo / data you need, ask me for exactly those and wait - never guess, and never answer with the placeholder text still in brackets.

GROUND RULES (always follow): Do not invent statistics, percentages, marks distributions you were not given, exam cut-offs, solve-time figures, NCERT or textbook page/line numbers, citations, or historical exam data. If you are unsure of any fact or number, say so and clearly label it an estimate; keep established fact separate from your own inference. Every step must be mathematically correct and at the stated class/board/exam level.
