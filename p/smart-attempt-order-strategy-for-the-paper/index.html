Smart Attempt-Order Strategy for the Paper

The exact order to attempt sections to grab easy marks first and protect the score.

Works on any free AI  ·  Best tool:  Any AI chat (ChatGPT/Gemini/Claude/Copilot)

✅ How to use this effectively

  1. 1. List your paper's sections and their marks.
  2. 2. Say which topics your class finds easy or hard.
  3. 3. Paste the whole block into the chat.
  4. 4. Read out the recommended attempt order.
  5. 5. Ask for a 'low-confidence student' version too.
🔧 If it is not right, reply with this: If the order ignores easy marks, reply: "Put the highest-marks-per-minute and most confident topics first. Re-rank the sections and explain the marks logic for each."
THE PROMPT
ROLE: Act as an exam-strategy mentor who teaches students how to sequence a paper to maximise marks and confidence.

CONTEXT: This is for [CLASS/GRADE] students sitting the [BOARD or EXAM e.g. CBSE / State Board / JEE / NEET] maths paper. Paper details: [LIST EACH SECTION WITH ITS MARKS, e.g. Section A MCQ 20 marks, Section B short 10 marks, Section C 18 marks, Section D long 20 marks, Section E case-study 12 marks]. My class is usually strong at [EASY TOPICS] and weaker at [HARD TOPICS].

YOUR TASK:
1. Recommend the best ORDER to attempt the sections, and explain the reason for each placement in one line.
2. Show which questions to do first (quick, sure marks) and which to keep for last (long, risky).
3. Give a clear rule for handling a question that looks hard at first glance.
4. Add a 'safety net' rule so a student never leaves easy marks unattempted.
5. Give a separate one-line tweak for a nervous or low-confidence student.

INPUT: My section and topic details are in the CONTEXT above. If marks are missing, assume standard values and say so in one line.

HOW TO WORK IT OUT: Internally estimate marks-per-minute for each section first. VERIFY twice: (a) the order really front-loads high marks-per-minute work, and (b) no easy section is accidentally pushed to the risky end. Never invent sections I did not list.

OUTPUT FORMAT (use these exact headings):
1) Recommended Attempt Order - a numbered list, each line: Section name - why now.
2) Do-First Quick Wins - 3 to 5 bullets.
3) Save-For-Last - 2 to 3 bullets.
4) The 'Looks Hard' Rule - 2 lines.
5) Safety-Net Rule - 1 line.
6) Nervous-Student Tweak - 1 line.

RULES: Concrete and ranked, never vague. Tie every recommendation to marks or time saved. Simple language a tense teenager will follow.

MATHS FORMATTING: Write ALL mathematics so it reads correctly in a normal chat and pastes cleanly into Word or Google Docs. Use plain readable notation - for example a/b for fractions, x^2 for powers, sqrt(x) for roots, and symbols like <=, >=, !=, +-, pi, theta, integral and sum. Do NOT output raw LaTeX or code such as \frac{}{}, \sqrt{}, x^{2}, $...$ or \( ... \) - these appear as broken symbols to a teacher in a free chat.

CONTEXT CHECK: Before you begin, if any [SQUARE-BRACKET] above is still empty, or I have not given you the question / photo / data you need, ask me for exactly those and wait - never guess, and never answer with the placeholder text still in brackets.

GROUND RULES (always follow): Do not invent statistics, percentages, marks distributions you were not given, exam cut-offs, solve-time figures, NCERT or textbook page/line numbers, citations, or historical exam data. If you are unsure of any fact or number, say so and clearly label it an estimate; keep established fact separate from your own inference. Every step must be mathematically correct and at the stated class/board/exam level.
